    My Neighbor Totoro

    Stats

    Released: April 16, 1988
    Preceded by: Castle in the Sky

    Followed by: Kiki's Delivery Service

    Written by: Hayao Miyazaki

    My Neighbor Totoro (となりのトトロ, Tonari no Totoro) is an animated film written and directed by Hayao Miyazaki, produced by Toru Hara and animated by Studio Ghibli for Tokuma Shoten.

    The film, which is set Tokorozawa City, Saitama Prefecture, tells the story of a professor's two young daughters (Satsuki and Mei) and their interactions with friendly wood spirits in post-war rural Japan.

    My Neighbor Totoro received critical acclaim and has amassed a worldwide cult following in the years after its release. It won the Animage Anime Grand Prix prize, the Mainichi Film Award, and Kinema Junpo Award for Best Film in 1988. It also received the Special Award at the Blue Ribbon Awards in the same year.

    The film and its titular character, Totoro, have become cultural icons. The film has grossed over $41 million at the worldwide box office as of September 2019, in addition to generating approximately $277 million from home video sales and $1.142 billion from licensed merchandise sales, adding up to approximately $1.46 billion in total lifetime revenue. The Art of My Neighbor Totoro was subsequently published by Tokuma.

    Plot

    In 1950s Japan, university professor Tatsuo Kusakabe and his two daughters, Satsuki and Mei, move into an old house in the country to be closer to the hospital where their mother, Yasuko, is recovering from a long-term illness. Satsuki and Mei find that the house is inhabited by Sootballs - small, dark, dust-like house spirits seen when moving from light to dark places. While the girls can see them, their father can't, however, an elderly neighbor (whom the girls can call Granny) tells them she used to see Sootballs when she was their age. Soon, they all get to work cleaning the house and unpacking. Later on, Granny's grandson, Kanta, visits with a basket but acts shy around Satsuki. When the girls become comfortable in their new house and laugh with their father, the Sootballs leave the house to drift away on the wind.

    The family goes to the hospital to visit their mom who is improving. The doctors say that she should be able to come home in two weeks. The next day, Satsuki starts class at her new school. While Tatsuo grades papers at home, Mei goes off exploring. Soon, she sees two white, rabbit-like ears in the grass and follows the ears under the house. She discovers two small magical creatures who lead her through a briar patch and into the hollow of a large camphor tree. She meets and befriends a larger version of the same kind of spirit, which identifies itself by a series of roars that she interprets as Totoro. She falls asleep atop the large Totoro, but when Satsuki finds her, she is on the ground in a dense briar clearing. Despite her many attempts, Mei is unable to show her family Totoro's tree. Her father comforts her by telling her that this is the spirit of the forest and that Totoro will reveal himself when he wants to. When they find the tree, the hole is gone. While Satsuki is disappointed, she is assured that she will see him if she's lucky.

    One day, Satsuki leaves Mei with Kanta's parents and Granny for the day. However, during the school day, Granny brings Mei to Satsuki's school. Mei refused to stop crying until she saw Satsuki, who tells Mei she has to stay with Granny since their father is teaching and she has school. Mei insists on staying, so with the teacher's permission, she sits next to Satsuki and colors, to Satsuki's chagrin. At the end of the school day, it starts to rain. The girls wait in a temple near the path until the rain lightens up. Kanta walks by and gives the girls his umbrella and runs home. At home, Satsuki works on her homework and Mei does some art. When their father is scheduled to return from work, the girls head to the bus stop to meet him, but he is not on the bus so they decide to wait, and it grows dark. As they wait, Mei eventually falls asleep on Satsuki's back.

    Totoro appears beside them. He only has a leaf on his head for protection against the rain, so Satsuki offers him her extra umbrella. Totoro is delighted at both the shelter and the sounds that raindrops make on the umbrella. He likes it so much he jumps and makes all the drops fall on the umbrella and lets out a roar which wakes Mei and the rain stops. Soon another bus comes, but it's a bus-shaped giant cat (known as the Catbus). In gratitude, he gives the girls a bundle of nuts and seeds. Totoro boards the bus, taking the umbrella. Shortly after, their father's bus arrives and he explains the train was crowded so he had to wait for the for the next bus. The girls excitedly tell him what they saw, and then they head home.

    Satuski writes to their mom and tells her everything that happened. The girls plant the seeds, but they aren't growing and Mei watches them every day. A few days later, the girls sleep outside in the heat and are awakened at midnight to find Totoro and his two miniature colleagues engaged in a ceremonial dance around the planted seeds. The girls join in, and the seeds sprout, grow, and combine into an enormous tree. Totoro takes his colleagues and the girls for a ride on a magical flying top. Afterwards, they sit in the tree and play a flute Totoro gives them. In the morning, the tree is gone, but the seeds have indeed sprouted, which means they weren't dreaming after all.

    Some time later, the girls help Granny harvest some vegetables and Mei wants to give her mom the corn she picked. The girls find out from a telegram that their mother's hospital wants them to call the doctor. Kanta takes Satuski to his uncle's house to use the phone, and Mei follows them despite being told to stay with Granny. Satuski contacts her father and says he'll call the number and get back to her. As it turns out, a planned visit by Yasuko has to be postponed because of a setback in her treatment. Satsuki, disappointed and worried, tells Mei the bad news, which Mei does not take well. This leads into an argument between the two, ending in Satsuki angrily yelling at Mei and stomping off. Mei starts to cry and Kanta walks with her. Back at the house, the girls both sit alone. Satuski worries that her mother may die. After seeing her sister upset, Mei decides to walk to the hospital to bring some fresh corn to her mother.

    Mei's disappearance prompts Satsuki and the neighbors to search for her. It starts to grow dark before she is found. Eventually, Satsuki returns in desperation to the camphor tree and pleads for Totoro's help. Delighted to be of assistance, he summons the Catbus, which carries her to where the lost Mei sits. Having rescued her, the Catbus then whisks her and Satsuki over the countryside to see their mother in the hospital. The girls perch in a tree outside of the hospital, overhearing a conversation between their parents and discovering that she has been kept in hospital by a cold and is otherwise doing well. Their mother still can't believe the doctor would send a telegram over something this minor. The girls secretly leave the ear of corn on the windowsill, where it is discovered by the parents, and return home on the Catbus. When the Catbus departs, it disappears from the girls' sight. Granny is happy that Mei is safe and Kanta apologizes for how he acted.

    In the end credits, Mei and Satsuki's mother returns home, and the sisters play with other children, with Totoro and his friends as unseen observers.
